# SECTION 2 — THE INTERVIEW PROGRAMME

## Logistics

**Recruiting.** Week 1 deliverable: the beachhead list (the ICP's cheapest asset — built from directories, communities, LinkedIn company search, agency award lists; target 1,000+ names to prove A6's density claim while creating the recruiting pool). Interview sample per the ICP: **20 total — 12 beachhead-exact, 4 adjacent (creative/larger agencies, testing the boundary), 4 scar-tissue (recent churners from Apollo/Instantly/AI-SDR tools).** Channels: warm intros first (highest show rate), community posts second (give-first framing: "researching how agencies really win clients — 30 findings shared back with participants"), cold outreach last (and written to our own standard — every cold recruiting email is itself a positioning test). **Incentives:** the research summary shared back (genuinely valuable to them), a £50 voucher or charity donation, and early access standing. **Format:** 60 minutes, recorded with consent, video where possible. **Note-taking:** a one-page structured sheet per interview — verbatim quotes (their words are marketing gold, per ICP §5), surprise register (what contradicted our documents), and assumption tags (each note tagged A1–A22). **Synthesis:** Friday of each interview week — affinity clustering against the register, ledger updated (Section 5), one "what changed this week" paragraph in the decision log.

## The seven guides (Mom-Test discipline throughout: past behaviour, never hypotheticals; their numbers, not their opinions)

**Guide 1 — Discovery & pain (A1, A6).** Walk me through how your last three clients actually arrived. When did you last worry about pipeline — what triggered it, what did you do that week? What would losing your biggest client tomorrow mean, in numbers? Rank your top three business problems right now — where does new business sit? *(Listen for: unprompted feast-famine language; concrete recent episodes vs. abstract agreement.)*

**Guide 2 — Workflow & research process (A5, A15).** Show me how you decided the last cold prospect was worth your time. How long did researching them take, and what did you look at? What did you wish you'd known before that first call? How often do you sit down to do new-biz work — what does that session look like? *(Listen for: actual research depth — do they already do what Xenia does, or nothing at all? Both answers reshape A5.)*

**Guide 3 — Sales process (A3, A20).** What happened after your last proposal went quiet? Who wrote your last cold email — show me it if you're comfortable. What outreach have you sent that you're proud of? Embarrassed by? *(Listen for: volume-desire vs. precision-desire in their own framing — A20's evidence accrues here.)*

**Guide 4 — Tool history & scar tissue (A4, A21; the scar-tissue four especially).** What sales tools have you paid for — walk me through each: why bought, what happened, why cancelled? What was the moment you decided to cancel? What would a tool have to show you in week one for you to keep it? *(Listen for: the graveyard narrative matching ICP §4; the week-one bar in their words — this calibrates the astonishment criteria from the buyer's side.)*

**Guide 5 — Pricing & value (A4).** What do you currently spend monthly on new-biz tools and activities, all-in? Have you considered hiring for sales — what stopped you, what would it cost? What's a new retainer client worth to you over its life? *(No Xenia prices mentioned in this guide — anchors are collected clean here; priced conversations happen post-concierge, per Section 7.)*

**Guide 6 — AI trust & framing (A10, A11, A21).** How do you use AI in client work today? Would you send an AI-drafted email under your name — why/why not? Then the framing test, scripted: two one-sentence descriptions read in randomised order (*"an AI employee that finds and researches your ideal clients"* vs *"sales intelligence that finds the clients you're actually right for"*) — record first reaction verbatim, then: which would you click, which would you distrust, why? Then persona probe: "the product speaks as 'Xenia' — a named colleague. React honestly." *(A10 and A11 resolve on twenty samples of this, not on our aesthetics.)*

**Guide 7 — Objections (run last, always).** What would stop you paying for this? What's the first thing that would make you cancel? Who else would need convincing? What's the question you'd ask that I haven't answered? *(Listen for: objections not in the ICP's forecast list — new objections are register candidates.)*

# SECTION 3 — THE CONCIERGE MVP

The programme's centrepiece (the ICP ranked it above every other instrument; the whole documentation series stakes A2/A3/A5 on it). Ten agencies from the interview pool, recruited at interview close.

## The weekly operation

**Intake (per agency, week of joining).** The DNA interview, run scripted-human per the C1 design (homework done first: their site, cases, visible clients — opening with informed observations per Document 06's prepared principle). Within 48 hours: the **DNA document** delivered — built on the Document 03 schema (ten categories, testing the six-category suspicion), rendered per Document 06's document-not-dashboard standard, endorsed section-by-section in a 15-minute follow-up call. *(A16 measured here: completion, duration, the "being seen" reaction verbatim, endorsement rate.)*

**Delivery cadence (three weeks per agency).** Monday 08:00: the weekly email — written in Xenia's voice (Document 06 §2, the C8 register), containing **five recommendations** (verdict + three reasons each) of which **two carry full briefs** (the complete B1–B8 document as a designed PDF — the shareable rendering, tested from day one) and one is the **visible exclusion** with reasoning. Wednesday: a two-line follow-up nudge (testing follow-up tolerance). Friday: the outcome check — one question per pursued prospect, one-tap-equivalent (reply with a word). Fifteen briefs per agency over the cycle, matching the ICP's instrument spec.

**Turnaround and cost accounting.** Brief production budget: 2–3 founder-hours each at start, timed weekly — the *manual baseline* that the automation gap (A7) is measured against. Every brief is produced **AI-assisted from day one** (models doing research and drafting internally, founder editing to standard): this is the silent productisation, and it generates the phase's most valuable dataset — **the edit log**: every change the founder makes to machine output, categorised by rubric dimension (Document 04's QA-delta, operating before any product exists). By week 4, the unedited-pass rate has its first real number.

**QA process.** Every brief scored on the Document 04 rubric before delivery; nothing below 16/20 ships; scores logged per dimension; fabrication check (every claim receipt-verified) is absolute — a concierge-phase fabrication reaching a design partner triggers the Sev1 honesty protocol *even now*, because the trust posture is being rehearsed, not just the research.

**Customer communications.** All delivery in Xenia's voice; all *programme* communication (scheduling, feedback requests) in the founder's own voice, explicitly separated — the design partners must experience Xenia as distinct from the founder, or A11's persona data is contaminated. **Revision loop:** the brief format is frozen for week one, then revised weekly from feedback and behaviour (which sections got read — asked directly; which got quoted back — observed), with each format version logged so astonishment scores can be compared across versions.

**What stays manual (deliberately):** scoring judgment, final edit, delivery, outcome collection — the parts where human quality guarantees the value test isn't confounded by early automation weakness. **What is silently productised (deliberately):** research assembly, draft generation, evidence collection — the parts where the machine-vs-standard gap *is* the measurement. The line between the two is the phase's engineering roadmap, written by evidence.

## What the concierge measures, explicitly

A2 (astonishment: bar criteria a–e recorded per first session and per week), A3 (pursuit: contacted-within-14-days per brief), A5 (the "obvious to a pro" risk: a per-brief question — "did any of this surprise you?"), A7's gap (the edit log and unedited-pass rate), A13 (observed reading order — asked and watched), A14 (real decline reasons collected verbatim → the chip taxonomy), A15 (cadence reactions), A17 (at least two deliberately honest thin weeks if they occur naturally — never manufactured), A18 (share events logged: who forwarded what, unprompted), and the pricing conversations of Section 7 (post-value, weeks 3+ per agency).

# SECTION 4 — THE EXPERIMENT BACKLOG

Twelve experiments, formatted uniformly. Owner is Founder throughout (Section 11 prices this). "n" values are honest about what small samples prove: direction, not statistics.

**E1 — Framing smoke test (A10).** *Hypothesis:* one of the two framings materially outperforms on click and reply quality. *Method:* two landing-page variants (identical but for headline framing), traffic from founder's LinkedIn + modest paid; email capture then the one-question reply ("what's going on with new business at your agency right now?"). *n:* ≥300 visitors/variant before reading. *Metrics:* visitor→email; reply rate; reply *quality* (does it describe a real pain?). *Go:* ≥8% cold conversion on the winner, framing direction consistent with Guide 6. *Kill/flag:* both variants <3% on warm community traffic — the message, not the framing, is wrong; rewrite before concluding anything.

**E2 — DNA interview usability (A16).** *Hypothesis:* the scripted interview completes in ≤40 min at ≥85% and produces the "no tool ever asked me this" reaction. *Method:* run as concierge intake; time it; count completions; log verbatim reactions. *n:* 10. *Go:* ≥8/10 complete, ≥6/10 unprompted positive reaction, DNA endorsed ≥8/10. *Kill/flag:* ≤5/10 endorse — the hinge moment fails; redesign before anything else proceeds.

**E3 — Astonishment (A2, A5).** *Hypothesis:* hand-made briefs meet bar criteria a–e in ≥6/10 first sessions. *Method:* concierge first sessions, criteria recorded live. *n:* 10 agencies × first session. *Go:* per the ICP's pre-registered thresholds. *Kill:* miss both astonishment AND pursuit after one honest format iteration → the wedge's central value hypothesis is falsified (Section 8's gravest gate).

**E4 — Pursuit behaviour (A3).** *Hypothesis:* ≥30% of delivered briefs are pursued within 14 days. *Method:* concierge tracking, Friday outcome checks. *n:* ~150 briefs across 10 agencies. *Go:* ≥30% pursued; ≥2 meetings booked across cohort within 6 weeks. *Kill:* <15% pursued despite positive astonishment scores — the praise-without-action signature, treated as falsification, not as "founders are busy."

**E5 — Willingness to pay (A4).** *Hypothesis:* the £249–£649 band survives post-value framing. *Method:* Section 7's sequence. *n:* 10 concierge + 10 interview-only. *Go/Kill:* per Section 7.

**E6 — Real-money commitment (A4's hard form).** *Hypothesis:* ≥5/10 concierge participants convert to paid founding commitments (£149/mo, card on file, cancellable). *Method:* the founding offer made at concierge week 3+. *Go:* ≥5/10. *Kill:* ≤2/10 despite enthusiasm — pre-registered as falsification (the ICP's "not a pricing problem" clause).

**E7 — Confidence vocabulary comprehension (A12).** *Hypothesis:* founders correctly order and interpret the four words. *Method:* five-minute card task inside concierge sessions: order the words, assign rough frequencies, react to sample sentences. *n:* 10. *Go:* ≥8/10 order correctly, interpretations within band intent. *Fix:* any systematic misread → revise vocabulary before it hardens into product.

**E8 — Correction loop prototype (A14).** *Hypothesis:* element-tap + chip correction completes in ≤10 seconds and feels like teaching. *Method:* clickable prototype mid-phase, using real decline reasons harvested by then; task-based test in week-4+ concierge calls. *n:* 8+. *Metrics:* time, completion, the acknowledgment reaction. *Go:* median ≤10s, ≥6/8 describe it positively unprompted. *Fix:* chips missing real reasons → taxonomy revision (expected; this is harvest, not exam).

**E9 — Share behaviour (A18).** *Hypothesis:* ≥1 in 3 agencies shares a brief unprompted within their cycle. *Method:* shareable PDF from day one; share events logged (asked casually, never incentivised — incentives contaminate the pride-currency reading). *n:* 10. *Go:* ≥3/10 agencies. *Flag (not kill):* zero shares → the organic growth loop assumption weakens; GTM's referral weighting revisits.

**E10 — Weekly brief engagement (A15, C8's premise).** *Hypothesis:* the Monday email is opened and acted on ≥60% of weeks. *Method:* concierge delivery telemetry + Friday asks. *n:* 30 agency-weeks. *Go:* ≥60% engaged-weeks; cadence complaints ≈0. *Flag:* consistent "too much/too little" → cadence learnability (A15) becomes a product requirement, not a default.

**E11 — Coach-partner probe (A19).** *Hypothesis:* ≥1 of 3 agency coaches/consultants approached will run a co-branded "Ideal Client DNA" session with their audience. *Method:* three warm approaches with a concrete offer. *Go:* ≥1 yes with a scheduled date. *Flag:* 0/3 → partnership channel deprioritised in GTM revision.

**E12 — Empty-week copy (A17).** *Hypothesis:* the honest-nothing message reads as integrity. *Method:* copy test in week-4+ calls ("if you received this on a Monday, react honestly") — tested as copy, never manufactured live in the concierge (deliberate scarcity would be dishonest data). *n:* 8+. *Go:* ≥6/8 respond with trust language. *Fix:* dominant "I'd feel short-changed" → the receipts-with-silence design needs a value floor (e.g., the closest-miss analysis becomes mandatory).

# SECTION 6 — THE DESIGN PARTNER PROGRAMME

**Phase structure:** the concierge ten (this phase) grow into the founding twenty-five (MVP phase, per Document 03's staging); this section governs both, written now so the first ten sign under the same regime.

**Selection criteria.** Beachhead-exact per the ICP definition (deviations logged as boundary experiments, max 2/10); niche diversity across the ten (e-com verticals, service mixes — cohort learning needs variance); the founder personally does new business (the persona we're testing against); feedback willingness demonstrated, not promised (they gave real answers in the interview); and the ICP's disqualifiers enforced *especially here* — no volume-merchants, however eager, because their feedback would optimise the product toward the customers we refuse (the learning-diet rule from Document 03).

**Expectations — the two-way contract, written and signed (lightweight, one page).** They give: the intake interview, weekly 20–30 minutes (the Friday call or async equivalent), decline reasons, outcome reporting, honesty over politeness. They get: the concierge service free (then the £149 founding rate for life if they continue), the DNA document regardless of continuation (value that survives exit — the offboarding promise rehearsed), named influence on the product, and first case-study position with approval rights. **Cadence:** Monday delivery, Wednesday nudge, Friday check (per Section 3); a monthly all-partners note sharing anonymised findings (community-building among the founding cohort begins now).

**Feedback structure.** Structured: the Friday three questions (What did you pursue and why? What was wrong or useless this week? What did you *almost* do?). Unstructured: an open channel (WhatsApp/email) with a response-time promise. All feedback tagged to register rows within 48 hours or it evaporates.

**Exit criteria — both directions.** They may leave any time, keeping the DNA document and full export (the Document 05 departure rule, rehearsed manually); an exit interview is requested, not required. We exit *them* when: two consecutive silent weeks (dead data poisons the cohort — one revival attempt, then a graceful pause), disqualifier behaviour emerges (volume demands, misuse per Document 05 §10), or the boundary experiment concludes (adjacent-segment partners exit with thanks when their evidence is banked). **Reference programme:** wins convert to named case studies with numbers (the GTM's fuel, per the ICP) under a standing agreement — approval rights theirs, founding-rate lock ours, and the ask made *at the moment of the win*, when pride is highest and the favour feels smallest.

# SECTION 7 — PRICING VALIDATION

**Sequence is everything (the ICP's anchoring rule): value first, numbers second.** No Xenia price is uttered to any participant before they have experienced concierge output or an equivalent value demonstration. Guide 5 collects *their* anchors clean (current spend, hire cost, client LTV); pricing conversations then run in concierge weeks 3+.

**Instrument 1 — Van Westendorp, post-value.** The four questions (too cheap / bargain / expensive / too expensive) asked about "Xenia as you've experienced her these three weeks." *n:* 10 concierge + the strongest 10 interview-only participants (shown a sample brief pack first — a weaker value demo, flagged as such in analysis). *Read:* the acceptable band's overlap with £249–£649.

**Instrument 2 — Framing A/B (the ICP's core pricing bet).** The same price presented two ways to matched halves: tool-framed ("£399/month, compared with your current tools") versus employment-framed ("£399/month — about a tenth of the junior hire you've been postponing; one right-fit client pays for two years"). *Metric:* acceptance shift between frames. *This is the test of the entire pricing strategy* — if the frame doesn't move acceptance, Document 02's pricing hypothesis loses its engine and the band likely falls.

**Instrument 3 — The founding offer (E6).** Real money: £149/month for life, card on file, cancel anytime, made personally at week 3+. The only instrument that spends their money instead of their politeness; its result outweighs both others combined.

**Packaging test (lightweight).** One plan versus a hypothetical two-tier split, probed conversationally ("if there were a cheaper research-only tier…") — *hypothesis: one plan wins* (Document 03's billing simplicity); we test only for violent disagreement, not preference nuance. **Discount policy, tested by refusal:** no negotiated discounts exist beyond the founding rate; when a participant asks (they will), the scripted answer is the premium posture ("the founding rate is the discount — it's the biggest we'll ever offer") and the *reaction* is logged as A20-adjacent evidence. **Refund policy:** 30 days, no quibble, stated proudly — trust posture as pricing feature (N6's spirit); its cost is accepted as marketing.

**Success metrics (pre-registered, matching the ICP):** the band survives with ≥50% of post-concierge participants; framing shift is positive and material; **≥5/10 founding conversions**. **Failure reading:** enthusiasm-without-conversion at ≥£249 is wedge-falsifying, not price-adjustable — written here a third time across the series because it is the finding most likely to be rationalised away in the moment.

# SECTION 8 — THE GO / PIVOT / STOP FRAMEWORK

**The gate meeting: Week 7, calendared on day one, decision rules below, sole admissible input: the ledger.** Present: founder, plus one outside forcing-function (an advisor or investor-figure whose written role is to read the kill criteria aloud and demand the evidence — the ICP's decision-11 discipline made human). Every kill criterion carries the same override rule: overridable only in writing, with reasoning, logged — and the override itself becomes a ledger entry with a review date.

**Gate 1 — The pain (A1).** *Continue:* ≥60% of beachhead interviewees rank pipeline top-3 with a concrete recent episode. *Adjust:* 40–60% — narrow further (perhaps the pain concentrates in a sub-segment: post-anchor-loss agencies, £500k–£1.5m band); re-run with 6 more interviews before build. *Pivot:* <40% but adjacent-segment signal strong → wedge redefinition (the ICP's runner-up analysis activates: recruitment first?). *Stop:* pain absent across all segments tested — the company thesis, not just the wedge, is questioned; pause and rethink at Foundation level.

**Gate 2 — The value (A2+A3+A5, jointly — the gravest gate).** *Continue:* astonishment ≥6/10 AND pursuit ≥30%. *Adjust:* astonishment passes, pursuit lags 15–30% → the brief persuades but doesn't move; investigate the action gap (drafts? confidence? timing?) with one format iteration + 3 more agencies before the MVP decision. *Pivot:* astonishment fails on "obvious to a pro" (A5) specifically → the intelligence targets change (deeper technical signals, competitive intelligence angles) — product pivot within the same wedge. *Stop:* both fail after honest iteration → the concierge has falsified the value hypothesis at a fortnight's cost, exactly as designed; the documents' core bet does not survive contact, and the honest outcome is a fundamental rethink, recorded without euphemism.

**Gate 3 — The money (A4).** *Continue:* band survives + ≥5/10 founding conversions. *Adjust:* conversions 3–4/10 with band pressure → founding price floors lower or scope trims (but see the ICP's falsification clause — adjustment has a floor). *Pivot:* WTP robust only in the adjacent larger-agency segment → beachhead moves upmarket one notch. *Stop:* ≤2/10 despite demonstrated value → pre-registered falsification; no rationalisation clause exists for this one.

**Gate 4 — The channel (A9+A10, softer).** *Continue:* framing direction clear + a repeatable recruiting channel produced interviews/partners at tolerable effort. *Adjust:* framing murky → run the larger E1 sample before marketing spend. *Pivot/Stop:* n/a at this gate — channel evidence this early adjusts plans, it doesn't kill companies.

**Standing tripwires (any week, no waiting for Week 7):** a concierge fabrication reaching a partner (Sev1 rehearsal — process halts until cause is known); two consecutive weeks of zero pursuit across the whole cohort (early Gate-2 alarm — investigate immediately); recruiting failure (cannot fill 10 concierge slots from 20 interviews + community in 3 weeks → A6/A9 problem, and itself gate-relevant evidence).

# SECTION 9 — THE WEEKLY OPERATING RHYTHM

The founder's week, designed against the honest hour-count. Estimated load at full concierge: **~35–40 hours/week on this programme alone** — which is the schedule, not a warning label; anything else the founder does this phase competes with evidence production and should mostly lose.

**Monday (delivery day).** 06:30–08:00 final QA + delivery of all agency emails. Then interviews or brief production. **Tuesday–Thursday (production and interviews).** Brief production blocks (deep work, timed — the manual-baseline clock always running); interviews at a max of 2/day (more degrades listening); Wednesday nudges out by noon. **Friday (the reflection stack — the phase's most protected time, ~4 hours).** *Evidence review* (60–90 min): ledger updated, every new datum tagged and cited. *Quality review* (45 min): the week's rubric scores, edit-log patterns, fabrication check confirmations — the Document 04 weekly review, running before the product exists. *Customer review* (45 min): partner-by-partner health (engaged? pursuing? silent-week count?), Friday check responses processed. *Decision log* (15 min): what was decided this week and why, append-only, one paragraph per decision — the log the Week 7 meeting and the post-research report are assembled from. **Sunday (30 min).** Next week planned against the register: which assumptions get evidence this week? Any P1 starving? (A P1 assumption two weeks without new evidence is a planning failure, flagged to itself.)

**The weekly metrics snapshot (five numbers, posted to the decision log every Friday):** briefs delivered and their mean rubric score; pursuit rate to date; unedited-pass rate (from week 3); interviews completed against the 20; founding conversions to date. Five, deliberately — the Document 03 critique's watch-five discipline, rehearsed.

# SECTION 10 — THE POST-RESEARCH REPORT (DESIGNED NOW, WRITTEN THEN)

The report that exists at Week 7, specified in advance so the phase collects what it must contain — and so the report cannot quietly become a persuasion document. Its audience is the founder's own gate meeting, then investors and early hires.

**Part 1 — The verdicts.** Every register assumption: belief before, evidence collected (cited), belief after, confidence move. A1–A6, A10, A16 get full treatment; the deferred (A7's measured gap, A22's untestability) get explicit *limitations* entries — what we still don't know, stated as plainly as what we do.

**Part 2 — The numbers.** The astonishment results per criterion; pursuit and meeting counts; the unedited-pass baseline and edit-log breakdown by rubric dimension (the automation gap, quantified for the first time); WTP band survival and founding conversions; framing and persona verdicts with verbatim evidence; the interview synthesis (pain ranking, language harvest, objection census); channel yield (recruiting effort per partner acquired).

**Part 3 — The recalibrations.** Every [calibrates] mark across Documents 03–06 resolved, revised, or explicitly re-deferred with reason: the astonishment thresholds, the acceptance/pursuit targets, the DNA category count, the chip taxonomy, the confidence vocabulary, the above-the-fold order, the cadence default, the pricing band. This part *is* the amendment feed for the governed documents — each recalibration tagged with the document and section it amends.

**Part 4 — The decisions.** The gate outcomes (Section 8) with the ledger citations behind each; the founder-decision backlog from Documents 02–06 (the accumulated ~45 open decisions), each now either decided-with-evidence, decided-by-judgment-where-evidence-was-silent (marked as such), or consciously deferred with a date. **Part 5 — The recommendation.** Go/adjust/pivot/stop, in one page, written to be defensible to a sceptical outsider using only Parts 1–3 as support.

**The report's standing rules:** written from the ledger and the decision log only (no retrospective memory); failures reported with the same typography as successes; and every number that moved from a document's hypothesis carries its before→after visibly — the series' honesty convention, closing its loop.

# SECTION 11 — FOUNDER CRITIQUE

**Where we are over-testing.** Twelve experiments, twenty interviews, ten concierge agencies, seven guides — for one founder in seven weeks. The arithmetic is the critique: interviews (20×~2.5h all-in) + concierge (10 agencies × ~15 briefs × 2.5h) + programme overhead is a ~350-hour phase squeezed into ~280 founder-hours. Something yields. The pre-agreed yield order: E1's paid-traffic arm (LinkedIn-organic only — paid A/B at this n is noise dressed as data anyway), E11 (three emails, keep, but zero preparation), E8's prototype polish (paper prototype suffices), and the concierge's brief count (15→10 per agency if drowning — pursuit-rate n suffers, astonishment n doesn't). What must never yield: the Friday reflection stack (unprocessed evidence is un-collected evidence) and the fabrication QA (rehearsing the trust posture is half the point).

**Where we are under-testing.** A7 — the automation gap — is the company's biggest bet and gets only *measurement* this phase, not resolution; the report must resist the temptation to spin "gap measured" as "gap closed." A22 (retention/compounding) is untestable and therefore silently absent from the gates — the Week 7 "go" will be issued with *zero* retention evidence, and should say so out loud. And the channel test is thin: recruiting-for-research is not acquiring-for-revenue; A9's real test is the MVP phase's founding-cohort recruitment, and the report must not over-claim from research-phase recruiting ease.

**What biases remain.** The founder interviews, produces, delivers, scores, and synthesises everything — confirmation bias is not a risk here, it is the operating environment. Mitigations that survive honesty: the pre-registered criteria (decided when disinterested), the outside gate-witness (Section 8), verbatim-quote discipline (their words, not paraphrase), and the scar-tissue cohort (recruited to disagree). Mitigations we pretend less about: "unprompted" reactions elicited by an eager founder are never fully unprompted; concierge quality *is* founder quality, so A2's pass says "this founder can astonish," not yet "Xenia can" — the honest chain is A2-manual → A7-gap → then the claim. And the warm-network partner pool skews agreeable; the report's Part 2 must state what fraction of partners were strangers.

**What could invalidate everything.** Three findings, none currently expected, all survivable only if seen early: pipeline pain that is *seasonal rather than chronic* (interviews in the wrong month could misread A1 in either direction — the guides ask about the last twelve months for exactly this); astonishment that decays by week 3 (novelty masquerading as value — why the concierge runs three weeks per agency, not one demo); and the praise-action gap (A3's kill line) — the single most common way products like this die, and the reason pursuit, not applause, is the phase's true north.
